Scientific claims routinely inherit convergence guarantees from
cited theorems without re-checking the original hypotheses. The
two-timescale actor--critic convergence theorem of
\citet{kondatsitsiklis2000} has been cited for twenty-six years as
the algorithmic account of tonic-phasic dopamine dynamics. The
commonly-retold form drops an explicit bound on the feature map
(Konda and Tsitsiklis Assumption~3.1(a)). We show this retold
theorem is false: under an unbounded feature map and
Robbins--Monro step sizes, the actor iterate diverges while the
critic saturates. A corrected statement under summability
hypotheses machine-checks in Lean~4 with zero \texttt{sorry} and
is strictly stronger than the original.

This is not an isolated case. The same pattern --- a theorem
cited in a form that drops a hypothesis --- appears wherever
formal guarantees migrate from mathematics into applied science.
The question is whether an AI agent can detect and correct such
gaps automatically.

\paragraph{Kairos.}
We present \sys{}, a self-evolving verification agent that
operates in a closed loop:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=0.1em]
\item \textbf{Claim extraction.} Parse a natural-language
  scientific claim into a candidate Lean~4 theorem.
\item \textbf{Verification.} Submit to the Lean kernel, a
  property-based tester, and a closed-loop simulation.
\item \textbf{Refutation.} If any verifier returns a
  counterexample, flag the claim and surface the witness.
\item \textbf{Correction.} Revise the theorem statement to
  accommodate the counterexample. Re-submit.
\item \textbf{Prediction.} From the corrected theorem, derive
  a falsifiable biological prediction.
\end{enumerate}
The loop is self-evolving: each refutation drives a hypothesis
revision, and each revision is re-verified from scratch. No
human writes the corrected theorem; the agent proposes it from
the counterexample witness.

\paragraph{Case study.}
We apply \sys{} to four candidate dopamine credit-assignment
rules from recent Nature papers
\citep{tang2024dynamic, greenstreet2025value,
markowitz2023spontaneous, sousa2025tmrl}. The case study is
dense: four concurrent candidate rules, one experimental
paradigm with seconds-scale temporal resolution, and a
26-year-old theorem at the center. \sys{} formally refutes
TD(0) as an account of the Tang et al.\ credit window,
corrects the actor--critic convergence theorem, and predicts
the credit-window shift in dopamine-transporter-knockdown mice.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=0.1em]
\item \textbf{Formal refutation.} The first machine-checked
  refutation of a candidate learning rule against an
  experimental feature of a systems-neuroscience paradigm.
\item \textbf{Corrected theorem.} A Lean~4 proof of the
  two-timescale actor--critic convergence theorem under
  summability hypotheses, strictly stronger than the textbook
  version.
\item \textbf{Self-evolving pipeline.} \sys{} as a reusable
  agent architecture for formal verification of scientific
  claims, with a concrete instance of the refutation--correction
  loop.
\item \textbf{Falsifiable prediction.} Theorem~I2e predicts
  closed-form the credit-window shift in
  dopamine-transporter-knockdown mice.
\end{enumerate}
